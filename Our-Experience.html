Our Experience

Our personal experience in the computational thinking module

Nurdaulet Askar

The Complexity coursework ties everything together. You revisit algorithms from earlier work and analyse them formally: counting exact steps, identifying patterns as input size grows, and expressing runtime in terms of n. You also learn to distinguish between loop structures (FOR vs REPEAT...UNTIL) and how they affect how easy or hard it is to reason about an algorithm's behaviour.
By working through the three COMP11012 courseworks, I built up a progression of skills that go from designing basic algorithms to analysing how efficient they are on large inputs.
The Kidney Donations coursework tought me how to think about matching problems. I learnt to describe an algorithm precisely enough that anyone could follow it step by step, even without programming knowledge. I also explored how the problem scales: finding all valid pairs requires checking every combination, and extending that to 3-way chains makes the search space grow much faster. This introduces the idea that how you structure an algorithm directly affects how long it takes to run.
The Graphs Galore coursework introduces graph abstraction, which is one of the most powerful tools in computer science. I learnt to represent real-world problems (colouring a map, seating people who dislike each other) as graphs, and then design algorithms that operate on that abstract structure. I also discover that two seemingly different problems can share the same underlying algorithm, which is a key insight in algorithm design.

Taken together, these courseworks tought me to design, describe, compare, and analyse algorithms, which are the core skills of computational thinking.
